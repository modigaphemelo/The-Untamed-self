Fanon: The Wound

The Ghosts in the Room · The psychiatrist who mapped colonization of the self

"Each generation must, out of relative obscurity, discover its mission, fulfill it, or betray it."

— Frantz Fanon, The Wretched of the Earth, 1961

Frantz Fanon (1925-1961)
Martinican psychiatrist, philosopher, revolutionary
Fought with French Resistance, then Algerian Revolution
Died of leukemia at 36, weeks after The Wretched of the Earth published
His work: How oppression gets inside us. How to get it out.

Nietzsche gives us the hammer to test our cages.
Fanon shows us who built them and why.
He maps the colonization of mind, body, and spirit—
and the decolonization required for true freedom.

The Colonized Mind

Black Skin, White Masks

Fanon's first book (1952) explores the psychological effects of colonialism: How the colonized internalize the colonizer's view of them as inferior. How they wear "white masks"—adopting the colonizer's language, culture, values to escape their own blackness.

Applied to personal cages: How many of us wear "successful masks," "productive masks," "normal masks" to escape our authentic selves? How many of our "good habits" are attempts to wear the mask of the "ideal person" as defined by systems?

The mask isn't just deception. It's survival strategy. But survival at what cost?

"The black man wants to be white. The white man slaves to reach a human level... What is called the black soul is a construction by white folk."

— Black Skin, White Masks, 1952

Fanon's insight: Oppression creates psychological distortions in both oppressor and oppressed. The colonizer sees the colonized as less than human. The colonized internalizes this view. Both are trapped in the lie.

Fanon's diagnosis of modern cages: "Your 'self-improvement' is often just wearing better masks. Your 'productivity' is internalizing the colonizer's value system. Your 'discipline' is policing yourself according to their standards. You think you're becoming better. You're becoming better at being colonized."

Fanon's Map of Internal Colonization

The Look

The experience: Being reduced to an object by another's gaze. "Look, a Negro!" (Fanon's experience in France).

Modern equivalent: Being reduced to your productivity, your appearance, your usefulness. The corporate gaze. The social media gaze. The self-optimization gaze.

The effect: You start seeing yourself through that gaze. You become your own surveillant.

The healing: Returning your own gaze to yourself. Seeing yourself as subject, not object.

Bodily Schema vs. Historical-Racial Schema

Bodily schema: Your natural experience of your body from within.

Historical-racial schema: How history/racism layers meaning onto your body from without.

Modern equivalent: Your natural bodily wisdom vs. how systems tell you your body should be (productive, attractive, disciplined, optimized).

The effect: You lose touch with your bodily schema. You experience your body through the historical-racial (or historical-productivity) schema.

The healing: Reconnecting with bodily schema. Listening to your body's wisdom beneath the layers.

Violence as Cleansing Force

Misunderstood as: Physical violence against others.

Actually means: The violence of saying "no" to colonization. The force required to break internalized oppression.

Modern equivalent: The "violence" of setting boundaries. Of saying no. Of walking away. Of rejecting systems that define your worth.

The effect: This violence isn't destructive. It's deconstructive. It breaks the internal chains.

The warning: Not all violence is equal. Fanon distinguishes revolutionary violence from the violence of oppression.

The Fanonian Body

Fanon was a psychiatrist who understood: Oppression is somatic. It lives in the body.

  • The tension of constantly wearing a mask
  • The fatigue of performing for the colonizer's gaze
  • The illness that manifests when the body rebels against its colonization
  • The "nervous condition" of living between two worlds—your authentic self and the mask

Your body keeping the score isn't just personal history. It's political history written on your flesh. Your tension patterns, your illnesses, your fatigue—some of this is your body rebelling against its colonization by productivity culture, beauty standards, success narratives.

Fanon in the Trenches

How his diagnosis applies to your fight:

On Your "Good" Habits

Fanon would diagnose: "These aren't just habits. They're internalized discipline. You've colonized yourself. You police your own behavior according to standards that serve systems, not your aliveness."

From A Dying Colonialism: "The colonial world is a Manichaean world." (Divided into good/bad, civilized/savage, productive/lazy)

Interpretation: Your productivity culture is Manichaean: productive vs. lazy, disciplined vs. undisciplined, successful vs. failing. You've internalized these divisions. Your "good habits" are often you playing colonizer to your own colonized self.

(See: The Habits That Hold You)

On Your Body's Wisdom

Fanon would diagnose: "Your bodily schema has been overwritten by historical-productivity schema. You experience your body as a project to optimize rather than a self to inhabit."

From his psychiatric work: Fanon used dance, sports, physical therapy to help patients reconnect with their bodily schema.

Interpretation: Your gut feelings, your tension patterns, your fatigue—these are your bodily schema trying to communicate beneath the historical-productivity schema. Trusting your gut isn't mystical. It's decolonizing your perception.

(See: The Body Keeps the Score)

On Your Thoughts

Fanon would diagnose: "Your mind isn't free territory. It's occupied by factory thoughts. Decolonization begins with identifying which thoughts are occupiers and which are native to your being."

From Toward the African Revolution: "Imperialism leaves behind germs of rot which we must clinically detect and remove from our land and from our minds."

Interpretation: Your mind factory isn't neutral. It produces thoughts that serve systems. Decolonizing your mind means clinically detecting and removing the "germs of rot"—the thoughts that make you complicit in your own domination.

(See: The Mind Factory)

The Decolonization Protocol

Based on Fanon's work, here's how to decolonize your self:

Phase 1: Diagnosis

Identify the colonizers in your life:

  • What masks do you wear? (Successful, nice, productive, disciplined, etc.)
  • Whose gaze are you performing for? (Corporate, social, familial, internalized ideal)
  • Which of your "values" were imposed rather than chosen?
  • Where has your bodily schema been overwritten? (Exercise as punishment, rest as laziness, pleasure as indulgence)
  • What thoughts feel like occupiers rather than natives?

Rule: Don't judge. Diagnose. This is clinical work.

Phase 2: Deconstruction

The "violence" of saying no:

Remove Masks

Practice being without your masks:
- Spend time alone without performing
- Do things no one will see
- Notice what wants to emerge when no one's looking
This feels violent because masks feel like survival.

Reclaim Your Gaze

Practice seeing yourself without colonizer lenses:
- Look in the mirror without judging
- Notice your body without optimizing
- Track your desires without filtering
The colonizer's gaze is internalized. Reclaim your own.

Detox Thoughts

Identify and quarantine factory thoughts:
"I should be productive" → Colonizer thought
"My worth equals output" → Colonizer thought
"Rest is laziness" → Colonizer thought
Create mental quarantine for these. Don't entertain them.

Phase 3: Reconstruction

Building native structures:

  • Bodily sovereignty: Move, rest, eat according to bodily schema, not productivity schema
  • Mental sovereignty: Develop thoughts that serve your aliveness, not systems
  • Relational sovereignty: Build relationships based on mutual recognition, not utility
  • Temporal sovereignty: Use time for what feels alive, not just what looks productive

This isn't going "back to nature." It's going forward to authenticity. Building a self that isn't colonized territory.

The Personal is Political

Fanon's crucial insight: Decolonization cannot be purely individual.

Interlocking Systems

Your personal cages interlock with others':
- Your productivity cage supports economic systems
- Your beauty cage supports consumer systems
- Your "nice" cage supports patriarchal systems
- Your independence cage supports neoliberal systems

Freeing yourself has ripple effects. It destabilizes the systems that cage others.

Collective Decolonization

Fanon worked with communities because:
1. Isolation reinforces colonization
2. Healing happens in recognition between decolonizing subjects
3. Systems change requires collective action

Applied: Your untamed self needs other untamed selves. Not as self-help group, but as decolonization collective. People who recognize each other as sovereign beings, not resources.

Global and Local

Fanon fought for Algerian independence while treating individual patients. He understood: Macro and micro are connected.

Your personal decolonization is part of a larger movement:
- Decolonizing time from productivity
- Decolonizing bodies from optimization
- Decolonizing minds from factory thoughts
- Decolonizing relationships from utility

This isn't narcissism. It's revolution at the cellular level of society.

Fanon's warning to individualists: "You cannot decolonize yourself in isolation. The colonizer thrives on dividing the colonized. Your personal liberation must connect to others' liberation. Otherwise, you're just rearranging furniture in your cell while the prison stands."

The Dying Revolutionary

Fanon died young, but his work lives:

Writing Against Death

Diagnosed with leukemia in 1961, given months to live. Fanon raced to complete The Wretched of the Earth. Wrote day and night, between treatments, knowing he wouldn't live to see its impact.

Finished manuscript October 1961. Died December 6, 1961, in Washington D.C., under FBI surveillance. Buried in Algeria, a country not yet independent.

The lesson: He wrote not for career, not for fame, but because the truth demanded expression. He wrote as a dying man to the living. Every word urgent. Every insight forged in the fire of mortality.

The Unfinished Project

Fanon's work remains unfinished because:

  1. Colonization mutates: From territorial to economic to psychological
  2. The internalized colonizer is harder to fight than the external one
  3. Decolonization is ongoing work, not one-time event
  4. New cages emerge even as old ones fall

Your untamed self continues Fanon's project: The ongoing decolonization of human being from systems that would reduce it to having, doing, producing.

Your Fanonian Practice

How to work with this ghost:

Mask Detection

Daily check: What mask am I wearing today?
- Productive mask?
- Nice mask?
- Successful mask?
- Competent mask?
Notice when it feels tight. Breathe beneath it.

Gaze Reclamation

Practice seeing without colonizer lenses:
- See your body without judging
- See your work without measuring
- See your rest without guilt
- See your joy without justification
Reclaim your gaze. It's your territory.

Collective Recognition

Find others on decolonization journeys:
Not to form a new system
But to recognize each other
As sovereign beings
Not resources
This recognition heals.

Fanon's Charge to You

"I am not a prisoner of history. I should not seek there for the meaning of my destiny. I should constantly remind myself that the real leap consists in introducing invention into existence."

This is the Fanonian contract:
History gives you cages, but doesn't own you.
Systems colonize you, but don't define you.
Your task: Introduce invention into existence.
Create a self that systems didn't anticipate.
