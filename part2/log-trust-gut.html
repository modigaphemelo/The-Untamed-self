Log: The Time I Trusted My Gut

Field note #72 · The wisdom beneath reason

"The body knows truths the mind hasn't learned to articulate yet. Sometimes it whispers. Sometimes it grabs you by the spine and says: Run."

— Written with the memory of a chill that had no source

Date: December 8th, 2025
Time: 11:42 AM
Location: A boardroom overlooking a city that would have eaten me alive
Status: All signs said go. My bones said stop.

This is not about intuition versus analysis.
This is about the intelligence that lives
in the spaces between thoughts.

The Cage of Pure Reason

I was raised to worship at the altar of rationality. Feelings were unreliable. Intuition was superstition. "Gut feelings" were what uneducated people had instead of data. My education was a decades-long training in distrusting anything that couldn't be measured, modeled, or monetized.

The Triumph of the Prefrontal Cortex

I mastered decision matrices, cost-benefit analyses, SWOT charts, probabilistic thinking. I could argue any position with logic. I could dismantle any emotional objection with data. I had turned myself into a beautifully efficient reasoning machine.

The problem: Machines don't have survival instincts. They don't get that chill up the spine when something is wrong. They don't feel the visceral "no" when all the numbers say "yes."

The evidence of my rationality was impeccable:

  • Promotions based on data-driven decisions
  • Investments that performed as projected
  • Relationships analyzed for compatibility scores
  • Life choices mapped on five-year plans

I was the master of my fate because I never left anything to chance. Or feeling. Or that unreliable, unscientific thing called "instinct."

Foucault's ghost murmurs: "You weren't thinking freely. You were thinking within the approved categories. Rationality, as you practiced it, was just another disciplinary technology—training you to ignore the knowledge that lives in your flesh, your nerves, your animal self. The most dangerous thoughts are the ones that don't fit the spreadsheet."

The cage was invisible: My own mind, trained to dismiss anything that wasn't itself.

The Offer That Ticked Every Box

The job was perfect. On paper.

30% salary increase. Executive title. Team of fifteen. Corner office. Equity. The company was growing 40% year over year. The CEO had praised my work extensively during interviews. The offer letter was generous, the benefits exceptional.

My spreadsheet said: YES in green, bold, 24-point font.

My friends said: "Take it!"
My family said: "Congratulations!"
My mentor said: "This is the next logical step."
My bank account said: "Please."

And then there was the feeling.

"It started as a subtle wrongness—like wearing shoes that fit perfectly but weren't yours. Then it became a physical sensation: a cold spot in my chest when I thought about saying yes. A literal chill, as if someone had opened a window to a place I didn't want to go."

— From the journal entry that night

I tried to reason with myself:

  • "This is just fear of change"
  • "You're sabotaging yourself"
  • "The data doesn't lie"

The cold spot remained.

I made more spreadsheets. More pros and cons. More projections. The rational case grew stronger. The physical unease grew deeper.

The Body's Intelligence

What I was feeling wasn't emotion. It was deeper:

  • A tightening in my shoulders when I rehearsed saying yes
  • A literal taste of metal in my mouth looking at the offer letter
  • A sleep that felt like drowning the night I almost accepted
  • A gut that literally clenched when I imagined my future desk

My body was speaking a language older than words. Older than spreadsheets. The language of survival.

Three days before the deadline, I woke up at 3 AM with absolute clarity: I cannot take this job.

Not "I shouldn't." Not "It might not be wise."

I cannot.

As if my bones themselves would refuse to walk into that building.

Saying No to Perfection

I declined the offer. Gave no "rational" reason because I had none that would satisfy the spreadsheet minds around me. Said only: "It doesn't feel right for me."

The reactions:

The Confusion

"But why? The numbers are perfect!"
"Are you getting a counter-offer?"
"Is it the salary? They'll negotiate!"
"This is career suicide."

My reasoning sounded like madness to them. Because it wasn't reasoning. It was something else.

The Immediate Aftermath

The moment I emailed the rejection: the cold spot in my chest warmed. The metallic taste disappeared. I slept deeply for the first time in weeks.

My body celebrated a decision my mind still questioned.

Six Months Later

The company collapsed. Not slowly. Catastrophically. A fraud investigation. Lawsuits. The CEO arrested. The "growth" was fabricated. The "equity" was worthless.

Everyone who took those "perfect" jobs lost everything: savings, reputations, years.

The realization: "My gut didn't know about the fraud. It couldn't have. But it knew something was deeply, fundamentally wrong with the energy. With the truth of the thing. My body detected the lie before my mind could prove it."

What the Ghosts Knew About Guts

Nietzsche's Animal Wisdom

"You thought you were rising above your animal nature with your spreadsheets. You were actually abandoning your oldest, wisest self. Animals don't get deceived by perfect-looking traps. They smell the wrongness. They feel the vibration in the earth. Your 'gut' is your animal self—the part that still knows how to survive in a world of hidden dangers."

Nietzsche wrote: "In the end one experiences only oneself." The truest self isn't the reasoning mind—it's the whole organism, with all its ancient, embodied wisdom.

Fanon's Embodied Knowledge

"Colonized people learn to distrust their bodies. Your education was a colonization of your senses. They taught you that real knowledge comes from books, data, authorities—not from the feeling in your belly when someone lies. Not from the tension in your shoulders around certain people. Reclaiming your gut is decolonizing your perception."

Fanon understood: The body remembers what the mind is taught to forget.

Modern Neuroscience

(No ghost, just science): Your enteric nervous system—your "second brain" in your gut—contains 100 million neurons. It communicates with your brain via the vagus nerve. It detects patterns, threats, and truths long before your conscious mind pieces them together. "Gut feelings" are actually processed information from a sophisticated neural network that evolution built for survival.

When your gut says no, it's not being irrational. It's being pre-cognitive.

The paradigm shift: My gut wasn't opposed to reason—it was a deeper form of reason. One that processes more variables, recognizes more subtle patterns, accesses older wisdom.

The Gut Literacy Protocol

After that experience, I began developing what I call Gut Literacy—the skill of interpreting the body's intelligence.

1

The Body Scan Decision

Before any major decision:
1. Imagine saying yes. Notice sensations.
2. Imagine saying no. Notice sensations.
3. Compare: Expansion or contraction?
4. The body doesn't lie about what nourishes or depletes you.

2

The Sensation Dictionary

Learn your body's language:
- Chest tightness = violated values
- Gut clench = danger/wrongness
- Shoulder tension = carrying too much
- Throat constriction = unsaid truth
Your body is already speaking. Learn its vocabulary.

3

The Rationality Check

When mind and gut conflict:
1. What does the gut know that the mind doesn't?
2. What subtle data might the body be processing?
3. What's the cost of ignoring this feeling?
4. Can I test the gut's hypothesis safely?

Gut + Mind = Complete Intelligence

I no longer choose between gut and mind. I use both:

  • Gut says: "This feels wrong" → Mind investigates: "What specifically feels wrong?"
  • Mind says: "This looks perfect" → Gut checks: "How does my body respond?"
  • When they agree: Proceed with confidence
  • When they disagree: Pause. Investigate. The disagreement itself is data.

The untamed self uses all available intelligence, not just the kind that fits on spreadsheets.

Your Turn: Reclaim Your Body's Wisdom

This isn't about becoming "irrational." It's about expanding your definition of intelligence to include the 500-million-year-old supercomputer in your nervous system.

The Memory Re-evaluation

Think of a past decision that went badly. Before you decided:
- Did you have any physical sensations you ignored?
- A "funny feeling"? A hesitation? A chill?
- Did you rationalize it away as "silly"?

Go back and honor that sensation. It was trying to protect you.

The Small Experiment

Next small decision (what to eat, which route to take, who to call):
1. Check in with your body first
2. Notice sensations without judgment
3. Choose based on the body's response
4. Notice the outcome

Start with low-stakes decisions to build trust in your gut's intelligence.

The Sensation Journal

For one week, track:
- Physical sensations during decisions
- What happened after ignoring/following them
- Patterns that emerge

Your body is already giving you data. Start recording it.

Remember: Your gut isn't magic. It's pattern recognition at a level your conscious mind can't access. It's your evolutionary inheritance saying: "I've survived things like this before. Listen to me."
